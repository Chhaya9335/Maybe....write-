Self Interview

"Are my choices really that bad??"

Sometimes, I sit alone, almost interviewing myself in my head — asking questions no one else dares to ask me. The biggest one is always: “Are my choices really that bad?” I don't ask this with bitterness, but with genuine confusion. Because so many times, I’ve chosen with love, with passion, with all my heart — and yet, the outcomes have been the opposite of what I hoped. I’ve never been the kind of girl who followed the crowd. I always followed my heart, even when it made no sense. But lately, I’ve begun to wonder — maybe that’s exactly the problem.

I remember the first thing I ever truly loved — sketching. I would spend hours blending pencils , trying to turn my emotions into strokes on a canvas. I thought it was my escape, my calling. But the more I sketched, the more I was told, “You’re not doing it right,” or “This doesn’t look professional.” Slowly, what I loved began to feel like a mistake. So I gave it up.

Then came photography. I told myself, "Maybe I’m not meant to create from nothing, maybe I’m meant to capture what’s already beautiful." I bought an old camera with the money I had saved, and wandered around the city, clicking pictures of sunsets, strangers, abandoned buildings — things that spoke to me in silence. But once again, people said, “There’s no story in these.” The camera went back in the drawer. Another dream tucked away.

And love? Don’t even get me started. The boy I once thought I would spend my life with — he turned out to be a lesson, not a partner. I gave him everything: my time, my trust, my heart. In return, I received silence, confusion, and eventually, betrayal. I thought I had finally chosen right. Turns out, I was just another chapter in his unfinished story.

One of the first major choices I made was my career path. I fell in love with a course — something creative, something that spoke to my soul. I studied hard, imagined myself walking into that classroom, meeting people who shared the same passion. But I didn’t get in. Maybe it was the competition, or maybe just bad timing. I don’t know. Instead, I was admitted into a completely different course — one I had never even considered, one I never liked. Throughout the years of studying it, I passed every exam, scored well, got praise from my professors — but deep down, I never enjoyed a moment of it. I never felt connected. I was doing well, yes, but not living well.

Then there was college — I had big dreams. I wanted to get into one of the best colleges in the world. I researched, imagined myself walking across beautiful campuses, taking photos, laughing in libraries, living the dream. I also had a list of places I never wanted to go — not because they were bad, but simply because my mind had already rejected them. Unfortunately, the universe had its own plans. I didn’t get into my dream college. Instead, I landed in one of the colleges I had promised myself I would never go to.

At first, I was heartbroken. It felt like another wrong turn. But slowly, the experience humbled me. The teachers there were not just knowledgeable, they were kind. They saw potential in me that I had forgotten existed. I made friends I never expected to make — people who challenged me, changed me, and taught me lessons about life, loyalty, and growing up. That place, which I once saw as a mistake, became one of the most important chapters in my story.

It’s strange, isn’t it? How we think we know what’s best for us — but sometimes what we avoid is exactly what we need. It makes me wonder: were my choices really bad, or was I just blind to the blessings in disguise?

Even outside academics, it’s been the same pattern. I’ve trusted people who didn’t deserve it. I’ve poured my time into friendships that drained me. I’ve chosen places to work where I thought I would shine, but ended up burning out. But even in those mistakes, I grew. I learned how to speak up. I learned how to say no. I learned the value of protecting my peace.

Sometimes I wonder if the problem is me — if I’m cursed to fall in love with the wrong things, the wrong people, the wrong paths. I try to convince myself that it’s okay, that mistakes are part of growth. But how many wrong turns before I find the right road? How many times can a person rebuild themselves before they stop believing in the blueprint?

But even as I question myself, a small voice inside me whispers, “You loved deeply. You tried. That counts.” Maybe it’s not that my choices are bad — maybe the world just isn’t gentle with hearts like mine. Maybe loving the wrong things teaches me how to recognize what’s truly right. Maybe it’s not about having a perfect taste, but having the courage to taste life at all.

So, when I look back, I realize something important — maybe my choices weren’t always wrong. Maybe they just didn’t turn out the way I planned. But they led me to experiences I needed. They helped me become someone stronger, more aware, more alive. But I wouldn’t want to erase the lessons. I wouldn’t trade the people I met, the strength I found, or the wisdom I gained. Life doesn't always give us what we ask for, but it often gives us what we need — even if we don't see it at first.

So, are my choices really that bad? Maybe not. Maybe they were just... human. And being human means choosing with hope, even when there are no guarantees. I still believe in my heart, even if it has led me down difficult paths — because those paths have made me who I am.

So, if this is a self-interview, and I have to give myself an honest answer, here it is: Yes, I’ve chosen wrong. More than once. Maybe I’ll do it again. But no, I’m not sorry. Because every wrong choice has brought me closer to understanding who I am — a girl who loves fearlessly, even when the world tells her not to.

And that, I think, is a choice I’ll never regret.
